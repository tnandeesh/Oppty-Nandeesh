Entanglement, Measurement and Decoherence

The superposition principle of states (see Chapter 14) applied to a composite system allows the existence of entangled states with no equivalent in classical physics. This concept was introduced by Schrödinger as early as 1935 and popularised by the thought experiment of ‘Schrödinger’s cat’. 1

The same year, Einstein, Boris Podolsky and Nathan Rosen put forward an epistemological argument claiming to reveal the incompleteness of the current quantum theory, now known as the ‘EPR argument’. 2 This argument was at the origin of numerous theoretical and experimental works that sought to provide scientific answers to the philosophical debate that had been raised. The result was not only an intellectual breakthrough but also the opening up of new fields of application centred on the transmission and processing of information, for example cryptography, teleportation and quantum computing.

Since most physical systems are not in pure states but in mixtures of states, we have to adapt the quantum formalism by introducing the statistical (or density) operator. It allows us to analyse a measurement process, that is, the way of extracting information, on a microscopic physical object, knowing that the measurement apparatus and the observer are of macroscopic size.

Here, to simplify, we restrict ourselves to two-state physical objects, whose description and evolution have already been examined earlier in the book.

Entanglement and Correlations Entanglement in a Composite System

Entanglement of states is a purely quantum concept, the consequences of which challenge the classical description of physical systems. According to Schrödinger, entanglement is ‘... not one but rather the characteristic trait of quantum mechanics, the one that enforces its entire departure from classical lines of thought’.

(a) Separable and (b) entangled states in a quantum well. Two non-interacting objects, represented by white and black balls, form a composite system. In (b), the state of each ball is not individually defined, although the state of the whole (a pair of balls with different colours) is well defined.

In the previous example, the entanglement occurs with two objects that can be distinguished by one of their intrinsic properties, represented here by the colour, white or black. It can also occur for indistinguishable objects, such as two electrons, two identical atoms or two photons. As we will see, one system can even be microscopic while the other is macroscopic; we can also have two mesoscopic systems.
